 p Grevy's zebra weighs 350 - 450 kg (770 - 990 lb). It lives in arid, open bushed
grassland and is adapted to survive droughts, as long as it can reach water every several
days. Grasses make up about 70% of the Grevy's zebra's diet. It also browses on shrubs and
trees. The Grevy's zebra has an open society in which females with their young and males
on established  a href=".. .. glosst.htm#territory" territories  a  are the focal points.
There are no permanent bonds between adults. They are solitary or live in a variety of
different associations that usually do not last longer than a few months. Some stallions
are  a href=".. .. glosst.htm#territorial" territorial  a , with territories of up to 12
sq km (4.6 sq mi). The resident stallion tolerates other stallions in his territory, but
they do not interfere with the resident stallion when he is mating.  p 
 p The Grevy's zebra formerly occurred from central  a href=".. .. country ethiopia.htm" Ethiopia  a 
to  a href=".. .. country somalia.htm" Somalia  a  and northern  a href=".. .. country kenya.htm" Kenya  a . It experienced a significant decline in the
1970's, apparently due to poaching to obtain the zebra's attractive hide for fashion. It
is now found only in northeastern  a href=".. .. country ethiopia.htm" Ethiopia  a  and
some reserves in  a href=".. .. country kenya.htm" Kenya  a . br 
 br 
Poaching of Grevy's zebra is no longer a threat, due to protection under  a href=".. .. glossc.htm#cites" CITES  a . Loss of grazing habitat and access to water, due
to competition with increasing herds of domestic livestock, is the primary threat to the
Grevy's zebra. Additional threats are due to reduced river flow, due to irrigation, and
uncontrolled tourism in reserves, which causes disturbance and destruction of vegetation.  p 
 p *** The distribution of Grevy's zebra is "wedged" between the ranges of
arid-adapted  a href="equuafri.htm" [African] wild ass  a  and water-loving common zebra.  cite ( a href=".. .. refk.htm#Kingdon1997" Kingdon 1997  a )  cite   p 
 p *** A young Grevy's zebra can walk within a half hour of birth and can run for short
distances within 45 minutes of birth.  p 
 p *** The Grevy's zebra is the largest of the wild  a href=".. .. glosse.htm#equid" equids  a .  p

 p In the past, the Grevy's zebra was found in the Awash Valley, the Ogaden region, and
northeast of Lake Turkana in  a href=".. .. country ethiopia.htm" Ethiopia  a , south into
 a href=".. .. country kenya.htm" Kenya  a  east of the Rift Valley and Lake Turkana,
north of Mount Kenya and the Tana River, and east into western  a href=".. .. country somalia.htm" Somalia  a . It apparently experienced a significant
decline in the 1970's. In the late 1980's it occurred in three isolated populations:  a href=".. .. country kenya.htm" Kenya  a , north of the Tana River;  a href=".. .. country ethiopia.htm" Ethiopia  a , on the east side of Omo River to Lake
Zwai, and  a href=".. .. country somalia.htm" Somalia  a . It has not been seen in  a href=".. .. country somalia.htm" Somalia  a  since 1973 and is presumed to be extinct
there. It is now restricted to  a href=".. .. country ethiopia.htm" Ethiopia  a  and  a href=".. .. country kenya.htm" Kenya  a , and possibly  a href=".. .. country sudan.htm" Sudan  a .

 p Poaching of Grevy's zebra skins for use in fashion was a major problem during the
1970's, especially until 1976, when Kenya passed protection laws. Poaching is no longer a
threat, due to protection under  a href=".. .. glossc.htm#cites" CITES  a . Loss of
grazing habitat and access to water, due to competition with increasing herds of domestic
livestock, is the primary threat to the Grevy's zebra. In addition, reduced river flow,
due to irrigation, has decreased the availability of surface water in parts of its Kenyan
range. In reserves, another threat results from uncontrolled tourism, which causes
disturbance and destruction of vegetation.  p 
 p The female Grevy's zebra weighs 350 - 400 kg (avg 385 kg) (770 - 880 lb (avg 847 lb));
  males weigh 380 - 450 kg (avg 430 kg) (836 - 990 lb (avg 950 lb)).   p 
 p Grevy's zebra lives in semi-arid bush grass mosaics with a preference for tracts of
  grassland growing on deep sand, hard-pans, sumplands and in areas where fire and elephants
  have degraded the dominant Acacia Commiphora woodlands.  cite ( a href=".. .. refk.htm#Kingdon1997" Kingdon 1997  a )  cite  It is able to live in deserts
  if there is permanent water within reach every 2 - 5 days (for lactating mares 1 - 2 days)
   cite ( a href=".. .. refv.htm#vandierendonck_devries1996" van Dierendonck &amp; de Vries
  1996  a )  cite . br 
   br

 p Close to 6 years (wild); 3 - 5 years (captivity).   p

 p Usually August or September, but mating and births can occur all during the year.   p 
 p A mare usually bears 1 young at a time. There are usually 2 years between births.   p 
 p Young are generally  a href=".. .. glossw.htm#weaning" weaned  a  at about 8 - 13
  months of age.   p 
 p Up to 18 years in the wild.   p 
 p Grasses make up about 70% of the Grevy's zebra's diet. It also browses on shrubs and
  trees.   p 
 p Female Grevy's zebras return to localized birthing areas that appear to be traditional.
    p 
 p The Grevy's zebra has an open society in which females with their young and males on
  established  a href=".. .. glosst.htm#territory" territories  a  are the focal points.
  There are no permanent bonds between adults. Females associate in nursing groups, males in
  bachelor groups and all classes may join up in large, mixed herds, with none of these
  groupings lasting longer than a few months. Aggression is infrequent except that  a href=".. .. glosst.htm#territorial" territorial  a  males assert their mating
  prerogatives. A solitary breeding stallion maintains a large territory and associates with
  mares only when they enter his territory. The most successful males win grassy territories
  close to water. Grass and water are major attractions, especially for lactating females.   p 
 p Some stallions are  a href=".. .. glosst.htm#territorial" territorial  a . The  a href=".. .. glosst.htm#territory" territories  a  are large for a  a href=".. .. glossh.htm#herbivore" herbivore  a : 2.7 - 10.5 sq km (1 - 4 sq mi) in
  northern  a href=".. .. country kenya.htm" Kenya  a   cite ( a href=".. .. refn.htm#nowapara1" Nowak &amp; Paradiso 1983  a )  cite .   p 
 p The  a href=".. .. glosst.htm#territory" territories  a  of the breeding males are
  the largest that have been found in any  a href=".. .. glossu.htm#ungulate" ungulate  a 
   cite ( a href=".. .. refd.htm#duncan1992" Duncan 1992  a )  cite . br
