 p  b Bats  b  are  a href=" wiki Mammal" title="Mammal" mammals  a  of the  a href=" wiki Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)" order  a   b Chiroptera  b ; sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"  a href="#cite_note-2" [a]  a   sup  with their forelimbs adapted as  a href=" wiki Wing" title="Wing" wings  a , they are the only mammals naturally capable of true and sustained  a href=" wiki Flight" title="Flight" flight  a . Bats are more manoeuvrable than  a href=" wiki Bird" title="Bird" birds  a ,  a href=" wiki Bat_flight" title="Bat flight" flying  a  with their very long spread-out digits covered with a thin  a href=" wiki Skin" title="Skin" membrane  a  or  a href=" wiki Patagium" title="Patagium" patagium  a . The smallest bat, and arguably the smallest extant mammal, is  a href=" wiki Kitti%27s_hog-nosed_bat" title="Kitti's hog-nosed bat" Kitti's hog-nosed bat  a , which is 29–34 mm (1.14–1.34 in) in length, 15 cm (5.91 in) across the wings and 2–2.6 g (0.07–0.09 oz) in mass. The largest bats are the  a href=" wiki Flying_fox#Physical_characteristics" class="mw-redirect" title="Flying fox" flying foxes  a  and the  a href=" wiki Giant_golden-crowned_flying_fox" title="Giant golden-crowned flying fox" giant golden-crowned flying fox  a ,  i Acerodon jubatus  i , which can weigh 1.6 kg (4 lb) and have a wingspan of 1.7 m (5 ft 7 in).
  p 
 p The second largest order of mammals, bats comprise about 20% of all classified mammal species worldwide, with over 1,200 species. These were traditionally divided into two suborders: the largely fruit-eating  a href=" wiki Megabat" title="Megabat" megabats  a , and the  a href=" wiki Animal_echolocation" title="Animal echolocation" echolocating  a   a href=" wiki Microbat" title="Microbat" microbats  a . But more recent evidence has supported dividing the order into  a href=" wiki Yinpterochiroptera" title="Yinpterochiroptera" Yinpterochiroptera  a  and  a href=" wiki Yangochiroptera" title="Yangochiroptera" Yangochiroptera  a , with megabats as members of the former along with several species of microbats. Many bats are  a href=" wiki Insectivore" title="Insectivore" insectivores  a , and most of the rest are  a href=" wiki Frugivore" title="Frugivore" frugivores  a  (fruit-eaters). A few species feed on animals other than insects; for example, the  a href=" wiki Vampire_bat" title="Vampire bat" vampire bats  a   a href=" wiki Hematophagy" title="Hematophagy" feed on blood  a . Most bats are  a href=" wiki Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal" nocturnal  a , and many roost in caves or other refuges; it is uncertain whether bats have these behaviours to escape  a href=" wiki Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator" predators  a . Bats are present throughout the world, with the exception of extremely cold regions. They are important in their ecosystems for  a href=" wiki Pollinator" title="Pollinator" pollinating  a   a href=" wiki Flower" title="Flower" flowers  a  and dispersing seeds; many tropical plants depend entirely on bats for these services.
  p 
 p Bats provide humans with some benefits, at the cost of some threats. Bat dung has been mined as  a href=" wiki Guano" title="Guano" guano  a  from caves and used as fertiliser. Bats consume insect pests, reducing the need for  a href=" wiki Pesticide" title="Pesticide" pesticides  a . They are sometimes numerous enough to serve as tourist attractions, and are used as food across Asia and the  a href=" wiki Pacific_Rim" title="Pacific Rim" Pacific Rim  a . They are  a href=" wiki Natural_reservoir" title="Natural reservoir" natural reservoirs  a  of many  a href=" wiki Pathogen" title="Pathogen" pathogens  a , such as  a href=" wiki Rabies" title="Rabies" rabies  a ; and since they are highly mobile, social, and long-lived, they can readily spread disease. In many cultures, bats are popularly associated with darkness, malevolence, witchcraft,  a href=" wiki Vampire" title="Vampire" vampires  a , and death.

 p The phylogenetic relationships of the different groups of bats have been the subject of much debate. The traditional subdivision into Megachiroptera and Microchiroptera reflected the view that these groups of bats had evolved independently of each other for a long time, from a  a href=" wiki Common_descent" title="Common descent" common ancestor  a  already capable of flight. This hypothesis recognised differences between microbats and megabats and acknowledged that flight has only evolved once in mammals.  p The 2003 discovery of an early fossil bat from the 52 million year old  a href=" wiki Green_River_Formation" title="Green River Formation" Green River Formation  a ,  i  a href=" wiki Onychonycteris" title="Onychonycteris" Onychonycteris finneyi  a   i , indicates that flight evolved before echolocative abilities. sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"  a href="#cite_note-21" [20]  a   sup  sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"  a href="#cite_note-22" [21]  a   sup   i Onychonycteris  i  had claws on all five of its fingers, whereas modern bats have at most two claws on two digits of each hand. This palm-sized bat had short, broad wings, suggesting that it could not fly as fast or as far as later bat species. This model of flight development, commonly known as the "trees-down" theory, holds that bats first flew by taking advantage of height and gravity to drop down on to prey, rather than running fast enough for a ground-level take off. sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"  a href="#cite_note-24" [23]  a   sup  sup id="cite_ref-Kaplan2011_25-0" class="reference"  a href="#cite_note-Kaplan2011-25" [24]  a   sup

 p The molecular phylogeny is controversial, as it points to microbats  a href=" wiki Paraphyly" title="Paraphyly" not having a unique common ancestry  a , which implies that some seemingly unlikely transformations occurred.  p The head and teeth shape of bats can vary by species.  p Microbats and a few megabats emit ultrasonic sounds to produce echoes.  p In low-duty cycle echolocation, bats can separate their calls and returning echoes by time. They have to time their short calls to finish before echoes return.  p In high-duty cycle echolocation, bats emit a continuous call and separate pulse and echo in frequency. The ears of these bats are sharply tuned to a specific frequency range.  p Bats may avoid flying during the day to prevent overheating in the sun, since their dark wing-membranes absorb solar radiation.
