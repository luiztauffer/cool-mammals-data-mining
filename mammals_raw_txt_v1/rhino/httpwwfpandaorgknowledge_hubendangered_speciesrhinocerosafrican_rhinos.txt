 p Just 150 years ago, Africa’s savannahs teemed with over a million  a href=" knowledge_hub endangered_species rhinoceros african_rhinos black_rhinoceros " black  a  and  a href=" knowledge_hub endangered_species rhinoceros african_rhinos white_rhinoceros " white rhinos  a . But relentless hunting by European settlers saw rhino numbers and distribution quickly decline. br 
 br 
Poaching also escalated during the 1970s and 1980s as demand grew for rhino horn, a prized ingredient in traditional Asian medicines - leaving both species at risk. br 
 br 
Thanks to vigorous conservation and anti-poaching efforts and an international ban on the trade in rhino horn, some African rhino populations are now stable or increasing. br 
 br 
However, most of the continent's remaining rhinos are found in just four countries – South Africa, Namibia, Zimbabwe and Kenya. Very few African rhinos now survive outside of protected areas and sanctuaries. br 
 br 
And poaching is again threatening the survival of some populations. br
