 p Abstract: Bat activity is highly seasonal in temperate regions such as the British Isles, due to variation in temperature and food availability, leading to hibernation being commonly used as an overwinter survival strategy. Outside the hibernation season, European species of bats of several genera ( em Myotis, Barbastella  em  and  em Plecotus  em ) are known to take part in social swarming behaviours. These typically occur in autumn and are thought to be associated with mating prior to hibernation. Only one British species, the Brown Long-Eared Bat ( em Plecotus auritus  em ) is known to swarm during spring. In this study, we used a combination of ultra-sonic acoustic monitoring and infra-red video surveillance, to describe seasonal activity patterns of Lesser Horseshoe Bats ( em Rhinolophus hipposideros  em ) across spring-autumn of two years; 2015 and 2016. Males examined during spring swarming were found to have well-developed epididymides, indicating that these individuals were in reproductive condition. This spring peak of activity is consistent with social swarming behaviour, and to our knowledge is the first record of swarming in this species and genus. We found no consistent evidence of equivalent swarming behaviour in autumn. In addition to the seasonal variation in activity, we detected positive effects of temperature and negative effects of rainfall on Lesser Horseshoe Bat activity, both during the spring swarming event, and across the study period.  p
